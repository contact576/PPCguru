2. Audit Scope

This audit reviewed every campaign, ad set and ad that spent, delivered or generated leads in either 30-day comparison window — not just the top performers — plus all currently-active entities regardless of delivery.

  • 66 campaigns in the account (5 currently ACTIVE; 8 had spend in one or both 30-day windows; 58 are dormant/paused).

Method. We pulled account-, campaign-, ad-set- and ad-level performance for each window; segmented by platform, placement, age, gender, region and device (one breakdown per query, then stitched — Meta silently ignores stacked breakdowns); reviewed creative copy, video-funnel metrics and Meta's own auction/quality diagnostics; and reconciled totals at every level.

Data-integrity checks passed. Campaign, ad-set and ad spend each sum exactly to the account total (CA$17,862.89 current; CA$15,301.09 previous); lead counts reconcile exactly (596 current; 565 previous). Reach and other unique metrics are not summed across entities (they overlap). Leads and on-Facebook leads (onsite_conversion.lead_grouped) are identical (596/596), confirming these are Meta Instant-Form submissions.

Two definitions to keep in mind. - Leads here = Meta-attributed Instant-Form submissions (7-day-click / 1-day-view window, with modelled iOS conversions). They are not the same as booked jobs, quoted customers, or revenue. Lead quality, appointment and sale outcomes cannot be confirmed from Meta data alone — they require CRM / offline-conversion data (see §24). - CPL = spend ÷ Instant-Form leads.

4. Executive Summary

Is the account improving or declining? Neither dramatically — it is holding volume while paying more per impression.

  • Leads are up +5.5% (565 → 596) on +16.7% more spend (CA$15,301 → CA$17,863).
  • CPL rose +10.7% (CA$27.08 → CA$29.97). Decomposed, the entire increase is a media-cost story: CPM rose +33.9% (CA$20.68 → CA$27.69). Every funnel metric actually improved — CTR +14%, click-to-lead 11.0% → 11.8%, leads-per-1,000-impressions +21%. The account is converting better but paying more to be seen.
  • Why CPM rose: budget shifted from the cheap, broad "All In One" water-softener campaign (CPM ~CA$17) toward the pricier HVAC + Water Heater CBO campaign (CPM ~CA$76). All In One fell from 47% → 40% of spend while HVAC-New rose 24% → 35%. That mix-shift alone raised blended CPM.
  • The recent 7-day dip (81 leads @ CA$33.27 vs 143 @ CA$30.39) is not a structural failure. It coincides with (a) pausing the WhatsApp and EV-Charger campaigns on ~17 July, which cut daily spend nearly in half (CA$106–252/day on 17–19 July), and (b) a new Ottawa campaign entering the learning phase on 20 July.
  • The cost-per-lead control did backfire — but it has already been removed. The older HVAC "CBO" campaign used a "Cost per result goal" (a cost cap ≈ the historical ~CA$30 CPL). With too few conversions to sustain it, delivery starved to roughly half budget (CA$555 total spend). The team rebuilt it as a "Highest volume" campaign, which restored delivery (CA$6,312 spend, 185 leads). No active campaign currently uses a cost cap.
  • Biggest single opportunity: the account leans on one ad — the Water Softener CA$29.99/mo video drove 278 of 596 leads (47%). It is fatiguing (hook rate slipping, 7-day CPL up to CA$30.53). Building 2–3 fresh water-softener creatives is the highest-leverage, lowest-risk move.
  • Biggest measurement gap: the GoHighLevel CRM pixel last fired 2025-11-01 and no custom conversions are defined, yet 6 of 13 ad sets optimise for QUALITY_LEAD (Conversion Leads) — a goal that needs a live lead-quality signal to work. Meanwhile the two ad sets that use plain LEAD_GENERATION have the lowest CPLs (CA$23–25).

Meta's own Opportunity Score for the account is 53/100. Its top-ranked recommendations align with ours: scale "All In One" (est. +52% conversions), fix ad-set fragmentation in HVAC-New (est. −27% cost/result), and increase budget on the budget-limited Tankless campaign.

6. Current vs Previous 30-Day Comparison

What the data shows. More money bought fewer, more expensive impressions, but those impressions converted better, so leads still rose. CPL = CPM ÷ (leads per 1,000 impressions) → current 27.69 ÷ 0.924 = CA$29.97; previous 20.68 ÷ 0.764 = CA$27.08. The math confirms: 100% of the CPL increase is the CPM increase; the funnel offset part of it.

Why it matters. If CPL were rising because the funnel was breaking (weaker CTR, worse form conversion), the fix would be creative/offer/form work. It isn't. The lever here is media efficiency — where and to whom the budget is delivered.

What Ecocare should do. Re-weight budget toward the low-CPM, proven engines (All In One water-softener ~CA$17 CPM; IG Reels ~CA$18 CPM ~CA$26 CPL) and reduce the drivers of CPM inflation (the fragmented, LAL-narrow HVAC-New ad sets at ~CA$76 CPM; the 65+ audience at CA$63 CPM; the Feed placement at CA$48 CPM). 11. Impact of the Cost-per-Lead Control — "Why the Cost-Control Change Backfired"

Background. You told us a cost-per-lead control (cost cap / bid restriction) was introduced and performance reportedly got worse. We investigated specifically.

What we found (with the caveat that the change-log tool is unavailable, so timing is inferred from bid-strategy fields, delivery and timestamps):

  • The affected entity is the original "EC | HVAC + Water Heater | CBO | PPC Guru" campaign (ID ending …888810472). Its bid strategy reads "Cost per result goal" — i.e. a cost cap set near the historical ~CA$30 CPL. Its sibling ad even carried the name "Bundles | BROAD | tCPA_$30," consistent with a ~CA$30 target being used.
  • Delivery starved. Over the previous-30-day window this campaign spent only CA$555 and produced 18 leads (CA$30.85) — roughly half of its CA$100/day budget actually delivered. Its ad sets were also set to QUALITY_LEAD, an even sparser conversion event, so the auction had far fewer than the ~50 conversions/week a cost-capped ad set needs to stabilise.
  • The classic failure mode: a cost cap set at (not below) the current CPL, on a campaign that doesn't have enough conversion volume to feed it, tells Meta to skip most of the auction → delivery slows, spend under-paces, the system can't find enough "cheap enough" leads, and volume collapses.
  • It has already been corrected. The campaign was paused (~3 June) and rebuilt as "…CBO | PPC Guru - New" using "Highest volume" bidding (created 2 June). The new version now spends CA$6,312 and produces 185 leads (CA$34.12) — delivery restored, at a CPM-driven CPL premium we address separately.

Conclusion. The cost control did backfire, in the textbook way: it throttled delivery because the campaign's conversion volume couldn't sustain a cap at the historical CPL. Crucially, it is no longer active — every currently-delivering campaign uses "Highest volume." Therefore the current CPL elevation is not caused by an active cost cap; it is caused by CPM mix-shift (§6/§10) and creative fatigue. We do not recommend re-introducing a cost cap now (see §12 for the conditions under which it could be re-tested).

23. Bidding-Strategy Audit

What the data shows. All active campaigns use "Highest volume" (no cost cap). Why it matters. With variable CPL and sub-threshold per-campaign conversion volume, restrictive bidding is the wrong tool right now — it will repeat the earlier delivery-starvation. "Highest volume" is the correct default while you fix structure and creative.

What Ecocare should do (recommended bidding plan): - Keep "Highest volume" on all campaigns for now. - Do not re-introduce a cost cap until: (a) a single consolidated campaign reliably produces ≥50 leads/week, (b) CPL is stable for 2–3 weeks, (c) the CRM quality signal is reconnected, and (d) any cap is set above the recent stable CPL (e.g. ~CA$33–35 if CPL settles near CA$28–30) and only after learning completes. - If/when tested, use a Cost-per-result goal (not a hard bid cap) on the one highest-volume campaign, and change nothing else simultaneously.
